1 Geometric Series — Convergence & Sum
Determine whether the series converges or diverges. If it converges, find its sum.
\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{e}{3^n} \]
Identify the Series Type
This is a geometric series. Factor out the constant \(e\) and write in standard form:
\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{e}{3^n} = e\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^n \]
So \(a = e\) and \(r = \dfrac{1}{3}\).
Apply the Geometric Series Convergence Test
A geometric series converges if and only if \(|r| < 1\).
\[ \left|\frac{1}{3}\right| = \frac{1}{3} < 1 \quad \checkmark \]
The series converges.
Find the Sum
For a convergent geometric series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n = \dfrac{a}{1-r}\):
\[ S = e\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^n = e\cdot\frac{1}{1-\tfrac{1}{3}} = e\cdot\frac{1}{\tfrac{2}{3}} = e\cdot\frac{3}{2} = \frac{3e}{2} \]
The series converges. Its sum is \(S = \dfrac{3e}{2}\).

Question 1 of 4
What two features of \(\dfrac{e}{3^n}\) identify this as a geometric series rather than a \(p\)-series?
Explanation: A geometric series is defined by a constant ratio between successive terms. Here \(\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \frac{e/3^{n+1}}{e/3^n} = \frac{1}{3}\) for every \(n\) — that fixed ratio is the defining feature. A \(p\)-series has the form \(\sum \frac{1}{n^p}\), where the variable is the base, not the exponent. Choice A describes a consequence of convergence, not a definition. Choice D correctly identifies the convergence condition but says nothing about why the series is geometric.
Question 2 of 4
What is the significance of the index starting at \(n = 0\) rather than \(n = 1\)?
Explanation: The starting index determines the first term \(a\) in \(S = \frac{a}{1-r}\). At \(n=0\), the first term is \(\frac{e}{3^0} = e\), so \(S = \frac{3e}{2}\). Had the series started at \(n=1\), the first term would be \(\frac{e}{3}\), giving \(S = \frac{e/3}{2/3} = \frac{e}{2}\) — exactly \(e\) less, which is precisely the missing \(n=0\) term. The index never affects convergence (Choice B), and no re-indexing is needed here precisely because we start at \(n=0\) (Choice D).
Question 3 of 4
Suppose \(e\) were replaced by an arbitrary positive constant \(c\). Which statement is true?
Explanation: Convergence of a geometric series is governed entirely by the ratio \(r\), not the leading constant. Since \(r = \frac{1}{3}\) regardless of \(c\), the series converges for any positive \(c\), and \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{c}{3^n} = c \cdot \frac{1}{1-1/3} = \frac{3c}{2}\). Choices A and C reflect a common confusion between the amplitude of terms and the ratio governing their decay. Choice D is wrong because \(c\) is a scalar multiplier — it cannot affect \(r = \frac{1}{3}\), which lives entirely in the exponent.
Question 4 of 4
If the ratio were \(r = \dfrac{3}{2}\) instead of \(r = \dfrac{1}{3}\), what would happen?
Explanation: The geometric series test is an if-and-only-if result: the series converges if and only if \(|r| < 1\). When \(|r| > 1\), successive terms grow in magnitude and the partial sums cannot settle to a finite limit — the series diverges. Choice B illustrates an important cautionary error: the formula \(\frac{a}{1-r}\) is only valid when \(|r| < 1\). Applying it when \(r = \frac{3}{2}\) produces \(-2a\), which is not a sum — it is a meaningless output of a formula used outside its domain. Choice D is wrong because the leading constant \(a\) never affects convergence.

2 Integral Test with Error Bounds
Use the Integral Test to show that the series converges. Then find the bounds for its sum.
\[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{4}{n^2+1} \]
Verify Integral Test Hypotheses
Let \(f(x) = \dfrac{4}{x^2+1}\). We need \(f\) to be:
  • Positive: \(f(x) > 0\) for all \(x \geq 1\) ✓
  • Continuous: \(f(x)\) has no discontinuities on \([1,\infty)\) ✓
  • Decreasing: \(f'(x) = \dfrac{-8x}{(x^2+1)^2} < 0\) for \(x > 0\) ✓
The Integral Test applies.
Evaluate the Improper Integral
Because the upper limit is infinity, we must write this as a limit before evaluating:
\[\int_1^{\infty} \frac{4}{x^2+1}\,dx = \lim_{b \to \infty}\int_1^{b} \frac{4}{x^2+1}\,dx = \lim_{b \to \infty} 4\Big[\arctan(x)\Big]_1^{b}\]
\[= \lim_{b \to \infty} 4\Big[\arctan(b) - \arctan(1)\Big] = 4\left(\frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\pi}{4}\right) = 4 \cdot \frac{\pi}{4} = \pi\]
Since the integral converges (to \(\pi\)), the series converges by the Integral Test.
Establish Bounds for the Sum
The Integral Test remainder bounds give, for the partial sum \(S_N\):
\[ S_N + \int_{N+1}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx \;\leq\; S \;\leq\; S_N + \int_{N}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx \]
Using \(N = 1\) to bound the full series sum \(S\) directly:
\[ \int_1^{\infty} f(x)\,dx \;\leq\; S \;\leq\; f(1) + \int_1^{\infty} f(x)\,dx \]
Computing: \(f(1) = \dfrac{4}{1+1} = 2\) and \(\displaystyle\int_1^\infty f\,dx = \pi\).
\[ \pi \;\leq\; S \;\leq\; \pi + 2 \]
The series converges by the Integral Test. The sum \(S\) satisfies \(\pi \leq S \leq \pi + 2\).

Question 1 of 4
Which of the following series could not be tested with the Integral Test?
Explanation: The Integral Test requires the corresponding function \(f(x)\) to be positive, continuous, and decreasing on \([N, \infty)\) for some \(N\). The series in choice B, \(\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n}\), is alternating — its terms are not all positive, so the positivity condition fails immediately. Choices A, C, and D all have positive, continuous, decreasing terms and are valid candidates for the Integral Test.
Question 2 of 4
We showed that \(\displaystyle\int_1^{\infty} \frac{4}{x^2+1}\,dx = \pi\). What can we conclude from this?
Explanation: The Integral Test is a convergence test — it tells you whether a series converges or diverges, not what the series sums to. The value \(\pi\) is the value of the integral, which is a continuous area calculation. The series is a discrete sum of infinitely many terms, and its value is a different (generally unknown) number. This is one of the most common misconceptions with the Integral Test. Choice D is also incorrect: the solution shows \(\pi \leq S \leq \pi + 2\), so \(S\) is actually at least \(\pi\), not less than it.
Question 3 of 4
The solution verifies that \(f'(x) = \dfrac{-8x}{(x^2+1)^2} < 0\) for \(x > 0\). Which Integral Test condition does this confirm?
Explanation: A negative derivative means the function is decreasing — so \(f'(x) < 0\) confirms the decreasing condition. The three required conditions for the Integral Test are positivity (\(f(x) > 0\)), continuity, and decreasing behavior. Each needs to be verified separately. Knowing that \(f'(x) < 0\) tells us nothing directly about whether \(f\) is positive or whether the integral converges — those require separate arguments.
Question 4 of 4
The solution concludes \(\pi \leq S \leq \pi + 2\). Where does the \(+2\) come from?
Explanation: The upper bound \(S \leq f(1) + \int_1^{\infty} f(x)\,dx\) comes directly from the Integral Test remainder inequality. The first term \(f(1) = \frac{4}{1^2+1} = \frac{4}{2} = 2\) acts as the "extra" amount above the integral. Geometrically, the integral underestimates the series (the curve sits below the rectangular bars for a decreasing function), so the series sum cannot exceed the integral plus that first rectangular piece of width 1 and height \(f(1)\).

3 Comparison Test
Use a comparison test to determine whether the series converges or diverges.
\[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n} \]
Choose a Comparison Series
Observe that \(n \cdot 3^n > 3^n\) for all \(n \geq 1\), so:
\[ \frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n} < \frac{4}{3^n} = 4\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^n \]
The comparison series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 4\left(\dfrac{1}{3}\right)^n\) is a geometric series with \(r = \dfrac{1}{3} < 1\), so it converges.
Apply the Direct Comparison Test
Since:
  • \(0 < \dfrac{4}{n\cdot 3^n} < \dfrac{4}{3^n}\) for all \(n \geq 1\)
  • \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{4}{3^n}\) converges
By the Direct Comparison Test, our series converges.
The series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{4}{n \cdot 3^n}\) converges by the Direct Comparison Test.
Alternative: The Limit Comparison Test with \(b_n = \left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right)^n\) also works cleanly, yielding \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \lim\limits_{n\to\infty} \frac{4}{n} = 0\). Since \(0 \leq L < \infty\) and \(\sum b_n\) converges, \(\sum a_n\) also converges.

Check Your Understanding 4 questions · read the solution first
Question 1 of 4
As \(n \to \infty\), which statement best describes the difference in behavior between \(\dfrac{1}{n}\) and \(\dfrac{1}{3^n}\)?
Explanation: \(\dfrac{1}{n}\) is a power function — its denominator grows linearly. \(\dfrac{1}{3^n}\) is an exponential function — its denominator grows without bound at an accelerating rate. For any fixed base \(b > 1\), we have \(b^n \gg n^k\) for all powers \(k\) as \(n \to \infty\). Concretely: at \(n = 10\), \(\frac{1}{n} = 0.1\) while \(\frac{1}{3^{10}} \approx 0.0000017\). These are fundamentally different functions — they cannot be treated as equivalent for comparison purposes.
Question 2 of 4
A student argues: "Since \(\dfrac{4}{n \cdot 3^n} < \dfrac{1}{n}\) for all \(n \geq 1\), and \(\displaystyle\sum \dfrac{1}{n}\) is the harmonic series, I can use it as my comparison." What is wrong with this reasoning?
Explanation: The inequality \(\frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n} < \frac{1}{n}\) is indeed true (since \(4 < 3^n\) for \(n \geq 2\), and the denominator \(n \cdot 3^n\) is larger than \(n\)). But the Direct Comparison Test for convergence requires the upper bound series to converge. Knowing a series is smaller than a divergent series is useless — zero is smaller than the harmonic series too, and zero converges; \(\sum \frac{1}{n^2}\) is also smaller than the harmonic series, and it converges. Being below a divergent series tells you nothing. The test only flows one way: smaller than convergent \(\Rightarrow\) converges; larger than divergent \(\Rightarrow\) diverges.
Question 3 of 4
Which series makes the most useful upper bound for \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n}\), and why?
Explanation: The key move is dropping the \(n\) from the denominator: since \(n \geq 1\), we have \(n \cdot 3^n \geq 3^n\), so \(\frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n} \leq \frac{4}{3^n}\). This gives a valid term-by-term upper bound. The resulting series \(\sum \frac{4}{3^n}\) is geometric with ratio \(r = \frac{1}{3} < 1\), so it converges — exactly what the Direct Comparison Test requires. Choice B is incorrect: \(\frac{1}{n^2}\) does not dominate \(\frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n}\) for all \(n\) — in fact \(\frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n} < \frac{1}{n^2}\) is not guaranteed — and in any case a \(p\)-series does not generally dominate an exponential term. Choice D fails because \(\sum \frac{4}{n}\) diverges.
Question 4 of 4
Which of the following correctly sets up the Direct Comparison Test to conclude that \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n}\) converges?
Explanation: The Direct Comparison Test for convergence requires two things stated together: (1) \(0 < a_n \leq b_n\) for all \(n\), and (2) \(\sum b_n\) converges. Only choice C satisfies both. Choice A has the inequality backwards — it claims \(\frac{1}{n} \leq \frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n}\), which is false. Choice B has the right inequality but pairs it with a divergent upper bound, giving no conclusion. Choice D has the inequality reversed (\(\frac{4}{3^n} \leq \frac{4}{n \cdot 3^n}\) is false since \(n \geq 1\) means the left side is actually larger) — this would set up a divergence argument if \(\sum \frac{4}{3^n}\) diverged, but it doesn't, so it's doubly wrong.

4 Alternating Series Test
Determine whether the alternating series converges or diverges.
\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} (-1)^{n+1}\frac{n+2}{n(n+1)} \]
Write in Standard Alternating Form
Let \(b_n = \dfrac{n+2}{n(n+1)}\). The series is \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}(-1)^{n+1}b_n\). The Alternating Series Test (Leibniz's Test) requires:
  1. \(b_n > 0\) for all \(n\)
  2. \(b_n\) is eventually decreasing: \(b_{n+1} \leq b_n\)
  3. \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} b_n = 0\)
Condition 1 — Positivity
For \(n \geq 2\), numerator \(n+2 > 0\) and denominator \(n(n+1) > 0\), so \(b_n > 0\). ✓
Condition 3 — Limit Zero
\[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n+2}{n(n+1)} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n+2}{n^2+n} = 0 \quad \checkmark \]
(The numerator is degree 1, denominator degree 2.)
Condition 2 — Decreasing
We can use the continuous analogue: let \(f(x) = \dfrac{x+2}{x(x+1)} = \dfrac{x+2}{x^2+x}\).
\[ f'(x) = \frac{(x^2+x) - (x+2)(2x+1)}{(x^2+x)^2} = \frac{-x^2 - 4x - 2}{(x^2+x)^2} \]
The numerator \(-x^2 - 4x - 2 < 0\) for \(x > 0\), so \(f'(x) < 0\). Thus \(b_n\) is decreasing. ✓
All three conditions of the Alternating Series Test are satisfied. The series converges.

5 Absolute vs. Conditional Convergence
Determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{\sqrt{n}} \]
Test for Absolute Convergence
Consider the series of absolute values:
\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \left|\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{\sqrt{n}}\right| = \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^{1/2}} \]
This is a \(p\)-series with \(p = \dfrac{1}{2} \leq 1\), so it diverges. The original series does not converge absolutely.
Test for Conditional Convergence (Alternating Series Test)
Let \(b_n = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\). Check:
  • \(b_n = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n}} > 0\) ✓
  • \(b_{n+1} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}} < \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = b_n\) — decreasing ✓
  • \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = 0\) ✓
By the Alternating Series Test, the series converges.
The series converges conditionally (converges, but not absolutely).

6 Ratio Test
Use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges.
\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{2^n}{n!} \]
Set Up the Ratio
Let \(a_n = \dfrac{2^n}{n!}\). Compute \(\displaystyle L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|\):
\[ \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \frac{2^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \cdot \frac{n!}{2^n} = \frac{2^{n+1}}{2^n} \cdot \frac{n!}{(n+1)!} = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{n+1} = \frac{2}{n+1} \]
Evaluate the Limit
\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{2}{n+1} = 0 \]
Apply the Ratio Test
Since \(L = 0 < 1\), the series converges absolutely by the Ratio Test.
The series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \dfrac{2^n}{n!}\) converges by the Ratio Test (\(L = 0 < 1\)).
This sum is related to \(e^2\): \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{2^n}{n!} = e^2\), so our sum equals \(e^2 - 1 - 2 = e^2 - 3\).

7 Root Test
Use the Root Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges.
\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \left(\frac{2n}{n+3}\right)^{n+1} \]
Set Up the Root Test
Let \(a_n = \left(\dfrac{2n}{n+3}\right)^{n+1}\). Compute \(L = \displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = \lim_{n\to\infty}|a_n|^{1/n}\):
\[ |a_n|^{1/n} = \left[\left(\frac{2n}{n+3}\right)^{n+1}\right]^{1/n} = \left(\frac{2n}{n+3}\right)^{\frac{n+1}{n}} \]
Evaluate the Limit
As \(n \to \infty\), the exponent \(\dfrac{n+1}{n} \to 1\), and:
\[ \frac{2n}{n+3} = \frac{2}{1 + \frac{3}{n}} \to 2 \]
Therefore:
\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\frac{2n}{n+3}\right)^{\frac{n+1}{n}} = 2^1 = 2 \]
Apply the Root Test
Since \(L = 2 > 1\), the series diverges by the Root Test.
The series diverges by the Root Test (\(L = 2 > 1\)).
We can also confirm divergence by noting that \(a_n \to \infty\) as \(n\to\infty\) (the terms do not approach zero), which alone implies divergence by the Divergence Test.

8 Radius & Interval of Convergence
Find the radius and interval of convergence of the power series.
\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{(x-3)^{2n-1}}{n^2} \]
Apply the Ratio Test to Find the Radius
Let \(a_n = \dfrac{(x-3)^{2n-1}}{n^2}\). Compute \(\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|\):
\[ \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{(x-3)^{2n+1}}{(n+1)^2}\cdot\frac{n^2}{(x-3)^{2n-1}}\right| = (x-3)^2\cdot\frac{n^2}{(n+1)^2} \]
\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty}(x-3)^2\cdot\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^2 = (x-3)^2 \cdot 1 = (x-3)^2 \]
Convergence requires \(L < 1\):
\[ (x-3)^2 < 1 \implies |x-3| < 1 \]
The radius of convergence is \(R = 1\).
Check the Endpoints
The series is centered at \(x = 3\) with radius 1, so check \(x = 2\) and \(x = 4\).

At \(x = 2\): \((x-3) = -1\), so \((x-3)^{2n-1} = (-1)^{2n-1} = -1\).

\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{-1}{n^2} = -\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2} \]
This is \((-1)\) times a convergent \(p\)-series (\(p=2>1\)). Converges.

At \(x = 4\): \((x-3) = 1\), so \((x-3)^{2n-1} = 1\).

\[ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2} \]
A convergent \(p\)-series (\(p=2>1\)). Converges.
Radius of convergence: \(R = 1\).
Interval of convergence: \([2,\, 4]\) (both endpoints included).

9 Power Series Representation — Geometric
Express the function as a power series centered at zero. State the domain, radius, and interval of convergence.
\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{1-3x} \]
Use the Geometric Series Formula
Recall \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{1-u} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} u^n\) for \(|u| < 1\). Substitute \(u = 3x\):
\[ \frac{1}{1-3x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(3x)^n = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 3^n x^n \]
Determine Radius and Interval of Convergence
Convergence requires \(|3x| < 1\), i.e., \(|x| < \dfrac{1}{3}\).

At \(x = \tfrac{1}{3}\): \(\displaystyle\sum 3^n\cdot\tfrac{1}{3^n} = \sum 1\) — diverges.

At \(x = -\tfrac{1}{3}\): \(\displaystyle\sum (-1)^n\) — diverges.

\[ \frac{1}{1-3x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 3^n x^n \] Radius of convergence: \(R = \dfrac{1}{3}\).   Interval of convergence: \(\left(-\dfrac{1}{3},\, \dfrac{1}{3}\right)\).

10 Power Series via Integration
Use properties of power series to find a power series representation for
\[ \int_0^x \frac{1}{1-3t^2}\,dt \]
Start from a Known Series
From Problem 9 with \(x\) replaced by \(t^2\), we have the pointwise identity:
\[ \frac{1}{1-3t^2} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 3^n t^{2n}, \quad |t| < \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \]
Integrate Term-by-Term
The term-by-term integration theorem for power series licenses integrating both sides over \([0, x]\) for any \(x\) in the interval of convergence. Using \(t\) as the dummy variable of integration and \(x\) as the upper limit:
\[ \int_0^x \frac{1}{1-3t^2}\,dt = \int_0^x \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 3^n t^{2n}\,dt = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 3^n \int_0^x t^{2n}\,dt = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{3^n\, x^{2n+1}}{2n+1} \]
Since the lower limit is \(t = 0\), every term evaluates to zero at the lower bound, so no constant of integration appears — the result is a unique function of \(x\), not a family of antiderivatives.
Write Out the First Several Terms
\[ = x + x^3 + \frac{9}{5}x^5 + \frac{27}{7}x^7 + \cdots \]
\[ \int_0^x \frac{1}{1-3t^2}\,dt = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{3^n}{2n+1}\, x^{2n+1}, \quad |x| < \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \]
Using a definite integral from \(0\) to \(x\) is the more principled formulation here. The term-by-term integration theorem is a statement about definite integrals on subintervals of the radius of convergence — the definite integral produces a unique function of \(x\) that directly matches the theorem's conclusion, without introducing an undetermined constant.

11 Taylor Polynomial Approximation
Approximate \(f(x) = \cos(x)\) by using the first four non-zero terms of the Taylor polynomial at \(x = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\).
Compute Derivatives at \(a = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\)
 \(k\)  \(f^{(k)}(x)\) \(f^{(k)}\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\)
0 \(\cos x\) \(0\)
1 \(-\sin x\) \(-1\)
2 \(-\cos x\) \(0\)
3 \(\sin x\) \(1\)
4 \(\cos x\) \(0\)
5 \(-\sin x\) \(-1\)
6 \(-\cos x\) \(0\)
7 \(\sin x\) \(1\)
The non-zero values appear at odd \(k\), alternating sign: \(-1, +1, -1, +1, \ldots\)
Write the Taylor Series
The Taylor series for \(\cos x\) centered at \(a = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\) is:
\[ \cos x = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(k)}\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)}{k!}\left(x - \frac{\pi}{2}\right)^k \]
Only odd powers contribute. Letting \(u = x - \dfrac{\pi}{2}\):
\[ \cos x = -\frac{u^1}{1!} + \frac{u^3}{3!} - \frac{u^5}{5!} + \frac{u^7}{7!} - \cdots \]
State the Four-Term Polynomial
The first four non-zero terms of the Taylor polynomial \(T_7(x)\) centered at \(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\):
\[ P(x) = -\!\left(x - \frac{\pi}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{6}\!\left(x-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^3 - \frac{1}{120}\!\left(x-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^5 + \frac{1}{5040}\!\left(x-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^7 \]
\[ \cos x \approx -\!\left(x-\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right) + \tfrac{1}{6}\!\left(x-\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)^3 - \tfrac{1}{120}\!\left(x-\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)^5 + \tfrac{1}{5040}\!\left(x-\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)^7 \]
Observe that this is exactly \(-\sin(u)\) expanded at \(u=0\), which makes sense: \(\cos\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+u\right) = -\sin u\). The series matches the Maclaurin series for \(-\sin u\) term-for-term.
